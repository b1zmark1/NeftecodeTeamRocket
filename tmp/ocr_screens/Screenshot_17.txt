ENGINE OIL ANALYSIS
Vehicle-aged oils were analyzed for several properties
including: fuel dilution, kinematic viscosity, metal content,
oxidation, total acid number (TAN), total base number
(TBN), and soot content. Analysis of the biodiesel fraction
and oxidation measurements were conducted at the Ford
Research and Innovation Center, and all other oil properties
were determined at Intertek. Intertek’s oil analyses were
‘based on in-house methods that are similar to the ASTM
methods. Fuel dilution determination was similar to ASTM
D3524, a gas chromatography (GC) method. Elemental
analysis was done similarly to ASTM D5185, and kinematic
viscosity was based on ASTM D445. Water concentration
was determined through Karl Fischer analysis, based on
ASTM 6304.

Oxidation was determined by differential IR analysis using
the fresh oil as a reference and the IR peak near 1700 cm™!
with a Nicolet Avatar 360 FTIR and OMNIC Integra analysis,
software. In the case of biodiesel-containing samples, the
interference of esters on oxidation was removed, as described
later. Oil oxidation products are composed of many different
carbonyl-containing compounds including esters, aldehydes,
ketones, and carboxylic acids. Oxidation can cause viscosity
increase, sludge formation, additive depletion, TAN increase,
and base oil breakdown. TAN and TBN were determined by
titration methods similar to those described in ASTM D664
and ASTM D4739, respectively. TAN refers to the amount of
acid species in the oil, and is an indicator of the potential to
have corrosion problems. TBN indicates the total base
components remaining in the oil to neutralize the buildup of
harmful acidic species in the oil.

The biodiesel fraction of vehicle-aged oil samples was
measured and compared with the use of two different
methods, a differential IR method similar to ASTM D7371,
and a second GC method. The differential IR method uses the
differential height of the ester peak near 1746 m7, which
grows linearly with increasing concentration of fresh
Diodiesel in fresh oil. Due to possible oil and/or fuel
degradation with aging or possible oil/fuel interactions that
could interfere with the IR peak at 1746 cm”, the biodiesel
dilution of selected samples was verified through a GC
method, using a GC column optimized for biodiesel

For quantification and peak identification, peak areas and

Typically, a biodiesel feedstock oil is comprised of
triglycerides containing a variety of different fatty acids, each
having different carbon chain length and/or number of
carbon-carbon double bonds in its chain. Typical biodiesel in
the US, made from soy oil, com oil, or animal fat, is
composed of FAMEs with carbon chain lengths of 16-18
carbon atoms, and 0-3 double bonds. The notation commonly
used is Cx:y, where x is the chain length and y is the number
of double bonds. For example, methyl linoleate (also known
as linoleic acid methyl ester) is identified as C18:2, indicating
acarbon chain length of 18 and 2 double bonds.
