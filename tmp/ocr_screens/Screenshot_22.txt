The method used here to determine the comected IR
oxidation values of biodiesel-containing samples allows for a
reasonable comparison of the oxidation level between oils
with ULSD and B20 dilution, and the method was applied
with caution after considering earlier findings that 1)
biodiesel degradation with increasing mileage was relatively
limited in GC analysis, 2) the oil oxidation with ULSD
dilution is minimal, and 3) a rapid increase in viscosity at
high mileage was not observed (shown later). Chemical
changes to the oil and/or fuel with aging and possible
interactions between them are not accounted for with the

relationship shown in Figure 11, and could explain why the
corrected oxidation values of some of the biodiesel-
containing samples are negative.

TAN and TBN

With either B20 or ULSD dilution, TAN values of fresh oil
and 12,000 mile aged oil ranged from 2 to 4 mz KOW g oil,
respectively (Figure 14). Generally, somewhat TAN
values were seen with ULSD dilution. With B20 dilution, the
TAN value stayed constant or decreased slightly for the first
~6,000 miles, prior to increasing after higher mileage
accumulation. The increase in TAN values at ~6,000 miles
occurs in the same timeframe as the increase in oxidation
values with B20 dilution.
