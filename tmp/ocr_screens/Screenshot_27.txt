For a given mileage, the wear was similar with B20 and
ULSD dilution when making a vehicle-to-vehicle pairwise
comparison. In a couple of cases (Vehicles #140 and #145),
wear was greater with B20 dilution near the end of the
recommended oil drain interval. For a given mileage, the
highest wear was with oil samples from Vehicle #142 with
B20 dilution. Oil samples from this vehicle had a higher than
average soot and iron content. Samples with ULSD dilution
were not taken from this vehicle, so a direct vehicle-to-
vehicle comparison can not be made in this case. The oil
samples from 144-2 (ULSD dilution) gave the lowest HFRR.
wear. The oil samples from 144-2 had the lowest fuel
dilution, highest viscosity, lowest soot concentration, and
lowest iron concentration for a given mileage. Any
combination of these oil properties could have contributed to
its relatively low HFRR wear. Given the variability of the
data, we can not conclusively determine that vehicle-aged oil
with B20 dilution gives higher HFRR wear than that with
ULSD.

Fuel dilution decreases the viscosity of the engine oil,
reducing its film thickness, and decreases the concentration
of oil additives. To isolate the effect of fuel concentration on
wear, a series of HFRR wear tests were run with fresh oil
doped with 0%, 16%, and 32% fresh B20 using the same oil
formulation that was used in fleet testing (Motorcraft SAE
15W-40, API CJ-4). A repeat test was also run with 32%
B20. Wear volumes from the two tests with 32% fresh B20
were 0.2 million um3 and 0.3 million um3, while vehicle-
aged samples with a similar fuel concentration had wear
‘volumes greater than 3 million um3. Unlike vehicle-aged oil,
the concentration of fresh fuel in fresh oil did not affect the
degree of wear Figure 22), which implies that wear under
‘boundary lubrication conditions does not increase solely from

increased fuel dilution, decreased viscosity, or decreased
concentration of oil additives. To better understand the wear

ism, changes in the chemistry of wear-protective
tribofilms with increasing oil age are investigated in the
Surface Analysis section.

In the HFRR tests with B20 and ULSD dilution, the friction
coefficient of vehicle-aged oil followed similar trends Figure
23). With both types of fuel dilution, the friction coefficient
typically decreased to a minimum level around 1,500 miles.
After 3,000 miles or more, the friction coefficient became
‘greater than that of fresh oil. Overall, the friction coefficient
ranged from 0.144 to 0.178, a 24% difference between
lowest and highest values.
