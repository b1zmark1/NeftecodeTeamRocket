FRICTION AND WEAR TESTING

Friction and wear protection capability of vehicle-aged oils
were evaluated with a High Frequency Reciprocating Rig
(HERR) from PCS Instruments. HFRR is a bench test to
study wear in the boundary lubrication regime. HFRR allows
for fast, inexpensive testing of a large number of samples,
and only requires an oil volume of 2 mL. In the bench test, a
non-rotating steel ball (6 mm diameter) slides against a
stationary steel disk (9.8 mm diameter). The ball material is
AISI E-52100 steel with hardness of 58-66 HRC, and the disk
material is AISI E-52100 steel with hardness of 190-210 HV
30. The rubbing interface is submerged in oil. The test
conditions used in this study were selected to represent
severely loaded engine contacts. They include a 1,000 g
normal load, 20 Hz reciprocating frequency, 1,000 micron
stroke length, and three hour test duration. The oil
‘temperature was 105 °C. The initial contact stress with a
1,000 g load is 1.3 GPa. A new ball and disk were used in
each test. Generally, oil with better boundary wear protection
‘will produce a smaller wear scar.

Prior to testing, the steel balls and disks were ultrasonically
cleaned in hexane for five minutes, and then rinsed again
with hexane. The ffiction coefficient and the electrical
contact resistance (ECR) are measured by HFRR during
testing. The reported friction coefficient and ECR values are
the average values over the three hour test duration. ECR
provides a measure of the formation of an electrically
insulating film at the sliding contact. It does not provide an
indication of film thickness. Zero percent ECR can be
interpreted as either metal-to-metal contact, or the presence
of an electrical conducting film. One-hundred percent ECR
indicates the formation of a fully insulating film between the
sliding surfaces. After the completion of testing, wear was
determined by measuring the volume of material that was
removed from a steel disk during a test using a Wyko
‘NT3300 optical profilometry instrument from VEECO and
‘Vision software program. The surface of each steel disk was

Fuel dilution in the oil (based on the ASTM D3524 method)
reached higher levels with the use of B20, compared to
ULSD Gigure 4). After 12,000 miles, the fuel dilution with
B20 and ULSD reached roughly 35% and 25%, respectively
At low mileage, the dilution levels were similar.
difference between B20 and ULSD dilution levels grew as Pa
miles increased, due to the slower evaporation rate of
biodiesel at the oil temperatures in this study. In addition, the
oil sump temperature with B20 was lower, which would
further reduce the evaporation rate. Other factors that were
described earlier, including the frequency of DPF
regenerations and the mass of fuel post injected into
cylinders, were seen to vary with fuel type, which also affect
the fuel dilution rate. Initial oil samples that were collected
right after an oil change contained ~5% fuel dilution, which
is partly attributed to carryover from the previous oil drain. It
is also possible that light ends of the oil were counted as fuel.
