CONCLUSIONS

Biodiesel usage will increase in the future and any impacts on
vehicles with diesel engines need to be characterized. In this,
study, several oil samples were collected from a fleet of
medium-duty vehicles equipped with an experimental
aftertreatment system to determine the effect of biodiesel
dilution on engine lubrication. The vehicles in the fleet used
post injection strategy to regenerate the DPF, which led to
high levels of fuel dilution in the engine oil. The general
findings are summarized below:

+ Use of B20 fuel increased fuel dilution more than ULSD,
mainly due to the slower evaporation rate of biodiesel at the
oil temperatures in this study. Of the total fuel accumulated in
the oil, the biodiesel fraction was ~40% wt. (B40) when
using B20. Engine oil viscosity decreased more with B20
dilution, due to its higher fuel dilution level.

+ Gas chromatography data was used to determine the
speciation of FAMEs, which allowed changes in the
feedstock of biodiesel sold at fuel stations to be tracked over
a period of time. With vehicle aging, the expected
concentration of the methyl linolenate (C18:3) fraction in the
biodiesel decreased because of its lower oxidative stability,
while that of methyl linoleate (C18:2) stayed relatively
unchanged. Methyl linolenate is the most oxidatively unstable
FAME present in typical biodiesel.

+ Use of B20 increased engine oil oxidation more than ULSD
as determined through IR analysis, but only minimal changes
in TAN and TBN were observed. Oil oxidation was
remarkably low with ULSD dilution after 12,000 miles, likely
due to the low bulk oil temperature of the vehicles.

+ With both types of fuel dilution, the iron concentration in
the oil increased with increased oil miles, but remained
within an acceptable level. However, lead concentration
increased more with B20 than with ULSD, indicating
increased lead corrosion of bearings.

+ Laboratory ball-on-disk wear tests (boundary lubrication
regime) showed increased wear with oil aging in vehicles for
both B20 and ULSD dilution. Wear with B20 dilution was
similar or slightly greater near the end of the recommended
oil change interval. However, the addition of fresh B20 to
fresh oil did not increase wear, suggesting that other oil and/
or fuel aging factors play a major role in wear protection
capability.
