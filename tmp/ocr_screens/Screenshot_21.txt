correspond with the change in biodiesel feedstock mix

biodiesel feedstock mix corresponded to increased oxidation
is difficult to determine, considering that it contained a lower
concentration of C18:3 which is most susceptible to
oxidation, as observed in GC analysis (Figure 9). We can
only speculate that the fresh B20 fuel from second biodiesel
feedstock mix had decreased oxidation stability, increased
TAN, increased contaminants, or some other less desirable
fuel property compared to the B20 fuel from the first
biodiesel feedstock. Alternatively, as is observed in biodiesel
oxidation stability testing, there may have been an induction
period before the oxidation commenced, e.g., corresponding
to depletion of antioxidants in the fuel or oil. The data in
Figure 9 suggest a steady rate of oxidation of C18:3
accumulated in the oil with increasing mileage. However,
