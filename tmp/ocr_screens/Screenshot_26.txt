Soot
Soot-containing engine oil has been shown to produce
increased engine wear2/. The normalized soot concentration
was similar in oil with B20 dilution and ULSD dilution
Figure_19). In both cases, the soot concentration steadily
increased throughout an oil interval, as expected. The soot
concentration was relatively low in all samples, and did not
exceed 1%. Slightly less soot was found in the oil of Vehicles
#144 and #145 than in the oil of the other vehicles, for both
uLsp and B20. Soot concentration determined through IR
lysis Figure 19) and through the Wilke method (not
ove here) followed similar trends. Even though B20 fuel is
known to decrease particulate exhaust emissions! it did not
greatly affect the amount of soot that collected in the oil
sump. This may be a result of the different size distributions
of soot particles that are found in the engine oil compared to
those produced in the combustion chamber. Smaller soot
particles are more likely to reach the oil through the small
clearance between the piston rings and cylinder bores than
larger particles, so the size distribution of soot particles that
reach the engine oil are expected to be smaller than the soot
particles produced in the combustion chamber.

FRICTION AND WEAR
HERR tests were run to are the wear protection
properties of oils with B20 and ULSD dilution under
boundary lubrication conditions. A 3D image of a typical
wear scar on a steel disk after a three hour test is shown in
‘igure 20. With both types of fuel dilution, wear increased as
oil age increased (Figure 21). The wear scar volume of oil
samples aged less than 1,000 miles were less than 0.5 million
m3, and that of oil samples aged 12,000 miles were
generally greater than 3 million um3. Several factors could
contribute to increasing wear, such as increasing fuel
concentration, increasing soot concentration, increasing
oxidation values, and decreasing TBN.
